<p>
  <b>Stan Woosley,</b> professor and chair of astronomy and astrophysics, and his<br>
  graduate student <b>Andrew MacFadyen</b> were featured in a <i>U.S. News and World<br>
  Report</i> story about gamma ray bursts. The article discussed new observations<br>
  that support Woosley and MacFadyen's theories regarding the origins of<br>
  gamma ray bursts. Their research is also the subject of an article on NASA's new <a href="http://science.nasa.gov/newhome/headlines/ast02nov99_1.htm">Space Science News Web site</a>.<br>
  <br>
  In a talk at the Missouri Botanical Garden last month, professor of biology <b>Lynda Goff</b> caught the attention of a reporter for the <i>St. Louis Post-Dispatch.</i> Goff described her research on parasitism in red algae as "Borg biology" (a reference to an alien race in the television series <i>Star Trek: The Next Generation)</i> because of the way the parasites take over and transform the cells of their hosts.<br>
  <br>
  <i>New Scientist</i> magazine's cover story on October 16 focused on efforts by UCSC researchers, led by professor of chemistry and biochemistry <b>David Deamer</b>, and another group at Harvard University to develop nanopore technology for superfast DNA sequencing.
</p>
<p>
  <b>Ellen Moir</b> of the New Teacher Center was on Pasadena radio station KPCC with California Education Secretary Gary Hart last week. . . . Also on southern California's airwaves was <b>Mike Urban</b> of politics, who was heard on Pacifica Radio's KPFK Radio in Los Angeles discussing Russia, the Chechen war, and Russian elections.
</p>
<p>
  Rounding out the southern California media wave was <b>Manuel Pastor</b> of Latin American and Latino studies, who coauthored an opinion piece for the <i>Los Angeles Times</i> about environmental justice. . . . More locally, <b>Pastor</b> participated in a Watsonville "visioning" forum on that community's future.
</p>
